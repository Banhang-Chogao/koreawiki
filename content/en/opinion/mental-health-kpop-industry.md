In 2019, when Sulli and Goo Hara died within weeks of each other, the K-pop industry promised change. Statements were issued. Mental health initiatives were announced. Agency
executives held press conferences with solemn expressions and vowed to do better. Seven years later, the question is unavoidable: what actually changed?

The answer, based on the evidence, is very little. Reports of grueling training schedules, severe diet restrictions, and inadequate mental health support continue to surface with
depressing regularity. A 2025 survey by the Korean Federation of Pop Culture and Arts Industry found that 68% of active idols reported symptoms consistent with moderate to severe
anxiety or depression. Only 23% said they had access to regular psychological counseling through their agencies.

## The Structural Problem

The fundamental issue is structural. The K-pop trainee system, which recruits children as young as 11 and subjects them to years of intensive training under competitive
conditions, is designed to produce flawless performers, not well-adjusted human beings. The pressure begins long before debut and intensifies exponentially after it, amplified by
social media scrutiny, demanding comeback schedules, and the precarious economics of an industry where most groups never turn a profit.

Some agencies have made genuine efforts. SM Entertainment employs a full-time psychological counseling team. HYBE has instituted mandatory mental health check-ins and limits on
weekly work hours. But these measures are voluntary, unevenly enforced, and exist within an industry that remains fundamentally extractive in its relationship with young artists.

The most recent controversy involves leaked internal documents from a mid-tier agency showing that trainees who reported mental health struggles were "deprioritized" for debut
consideration. The agency has since apologized, but the documents reveal an attitude that industry watchdogs say is widespread: mental health challenges are seen as a liability to
be managed or hidden, not a health issue to be treated.

As fans, journalists, and consumers, we bear some responsibility too. The demand for constant content, the scrutiny of every weight fluctuation, the culture of toxic fandom that
targets both artists and fellow fans — these are not forces external to the industry. They are forces we participate in.

Real change will require more than agency statements. It will require regulatory oversight of trainee welfare standards, mandatory counseling provisions, legal limits on working
hours for minor trainees, and a cultural shift that values artist wellbeing over content output. Until then, every promise of reform is just another press release.
